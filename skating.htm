Skating reduces your impact force to 1/10 -1/4 as reported by Mahar, et. al. This likely happens since there is no "flight phase" during skating and you apply s almost entirely in a horizontal direction. This differs from walking, which has both a horizontal and vertical component.



This model demonstrates non-ballistic motion from a side-view perspective for skating. There are several observations:
  1. The pelvis is at an angle with the legs, creating a shock absorber.
  2. The knees are also at an angle, creating another shock absorber.
  3. At the base of the spine, where the connect the sacrum to the pelvis, there is no torsion or intra-pelvic torsion.
  4. There is a reduction in the gravitational force, because the foot is not falling forward.
  5. Momentum is transfered between strides, thus easing the force necessary to propel forward.
