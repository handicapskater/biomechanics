Chronic Pain, Controlled Rolling, and a Mobility Aid Nobody Wanted to Recognize

Chronic musculoskeletal (MSK) pain from pelvic, sacroiliac (SI), hip, and spine loading makes walking burden-dense, while using skates as controlled rolling changes the movement pattern.

Chronic pain forced me to become a systems engineer of my own mobility. HandicapSkater documents the lived problem, the movement mechanics, the recognition record, and the data evidence behind controlled rolling.

Why this site exists

Walking and Skating Are Not the Same Movement Pattern

HandicapSkater documents a simple but difficult fact: walking and skating are not the same movement pattern, as discussed in the biomechanics. For my body, controlled rolling can be more tolerable, more repeatable, and more functional than walking.

The motorcycle with skates moment

One scene explains why this case matters: I leave a store wearing skates as a mobility aid, use a disabled parking space, get on a motorcycle, and ride away. Many people saw a stunt. The record shows an adaptation.

I began riding a motorcycle with skates because public transportation repeatedly refused access to the mobility aid I used to avoid high burden walking. I still had to get to work, appointments, groceries, court, and public life. When the accessible path was blocked, I built another one.

That moment exposes the platform problem: current systems cannot easily classify a disabled person shopping with skates used as a mobility aid, transferring onto a motorcycle, and using the same device for mobility, transportation, and access.

The core distinction

Movement Mode Changes Burden

Walking

Ballistic stepping: heel strike, single-leg stance, toe-off, repeated pelvic/SI load transfer. Walking is called controlled falling (scientifically: inverted-pendulum gait), where you project your foot and fall forward with catching yourself on your foot.

Skating

Controlled rolling: push, glide, rolling support, is a different MSK load path. Skating is propelled gliding locomotion, where there is more conservation of momentum.

Transportation

Passive exposure: bus/van shock, upright seated loads the sacroiliac joints, which act as shock-absorbers with low active control (i.e. the ability to move around to alleviate stress build up).
