Mobility data for a disability standard categories missed

HandicapSkater is a real world case study showing how a non-traditional mobility aid became visible through medical records, accommodation history, GPS routes, WHOOP context, Strava mobility records, Kubios/Polar H10 HRV/RRI testing, accelerometer evidence, and source linked data science.

The core issue is simple: for my documented pelvic, sacroiliac, and hip impairment, walking can impose painful ballistic burden while controlled inline skating provides functional mobility. The evidence supports individualized review. It is not a medical diagnosis, legal finding, or automated proof of pain by itself.

The category failure

People see inline skates and assume recreation. My record shows something different: prosthetic inline skates function as a non-traditional mobility aid because they reduce the walking burden created by a documented pelvic/SI/hip impairment.

Not ordinary walking

For my body, walking is not simply low activity. It can mean repeated vertical loading, pelvic/SI stress, cardiovascular escalation, and reduced functional range.

Not merely recreation

Repeated routes, accommodation records, public use history, and wearable data show a functional mobility pattern, not isolated recreation.

Not diagnosis by algorithm

Wearable data supports review. It does not diagnose pain, decide disability status, or replace medical and factual context.

The motorcycle with skates moment

One scene explains why this case matters: I leave a store wearing prosthetic skates, use a disabled parking space, get on a motorcycle, and ride away. Many people saw a stunt. The record shows an adaptation.

I began riding a motorcycle with skates because public transportation repeatedly refused access to the mobility aid I used to avoid painful walking. I still had to get to work, appointments, groceries, court, and public life. When the accessible path was blocked, I built another one.

That moment exposes the platform problem: current systems cannot easily classify a disabled person shopping with prosthetic skates, transferring onto a motorcycle, and using the same device for mobility, transportation, and access.

Beyond step counting

Most wearables count activity. This project asks the harder question: is the movement sustainable, harmful, accessible, or forced?

For disability, more steps are not always better. The goal is functional mobility, not conformity to walking.
