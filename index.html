INDIVIDUAL WITHIN-PERSON MOBILITY CASE STUDY

HandicapSkater

One person's long-running record of how walking and controlled inline skating can function very differently in the same body.

After pelvic and sacroiliac-joint injuries, walking became painful and difficult to sustain. Inline skates became a mobility strategy that connected movement, transportation, and daily access.

Wearable records, routes, episodic measurements, repeated protocols, photographs, and documentary history now make that lived comparison inspectable—without turning one person's result into a universal claim.

What Looks Like a Stunt Is the Access Story

A short public video shows the adaptation in one scene: shopping on skates, rolling to a motorcycle, and riding away without changing footwear.

What can look like performance is also a response to walking difficulty, balance, the burden of bending to change footwear, transportation barriers, and more than thirty years of adapting movement to real environments.

Appearance alone cannot show what the aid enables or what removing it would cost.

Why It Matters

Function Before Appearance

Unfamiliar appearance is not evidence that an aid lacks function. Review should begin with what the aid enables for the individual, what happens when it is removed, and what the actual environment requires.

Risk should be evaluated with individualized evidence rather than assumption. Effective alternatives should preserve meaningful access, not merely substitute a familiar device or service.

Generalized standards, evidence-review guidance, and direct-threat analysis live on HandicapSkater.org; this site supplies the personal case record those methods can help reviewers understand.

Evidence boundary

Wearables estimate physiologic, autonomic, musculoskeletal, movement, impact, and transport burden. They do not directly measure pain. This is within-person evidence, not diagnosis, causation, universal proof, universal safety, or a categorical legal conclusion.
