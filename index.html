Wearable health evidence for mobility and accommodation

Mobility data for bodies that standard categories miss

HandicapSkater is a real world case study showing how wearable signals, route data, HRV/RRI testing, accelerometer evidence, and source linked activity labels can help reviewers understand functional mobility burden.

The record supports individualized review. It is not a medical diagnosis, legal finding, or automated proof of pain by itself.

The category failure

People see skates and assume recreation. The record shows something different: prosthetic inline skates function as a non-traditional mobility aid that reduces the painful burden of walking.

Not ordinary walking

Walking can create pain, stress, and cumulative burden. The useful question is not how many steps were taken, but which mobility mode is sustainable.

Not merely recreation

Repeated routes, daily access patterns, and source records help show a functional mobility pattern, not isolated recreation.

Not diagnosis by algorithm

Wearable data supports review. It does not diagnose pain, decide legal status, or replace medical and factual context.

The motorcycle-with-skates moment

One scene explains why this case matters: I leave a store wearing prosthetic skates, use a handicap parking space, get on a motorcycle, and ride away. People saw a stunt. The disability record shows a workaround.

I began riding a motorcycle with skates because public transportation repeatedly refused access to my mobility aid. I still had to get to work, pay taxes, attend appointments, and participate in public life while fighting systems that treated the mobility aid as the problem.

The viral reaction matters because it reveals the platform problem: current categories cannot easily describe shopping with prosthetic skates, parking as a disabled person, transferring onto a motorcycle, and riding away with the same assistive device.

Beyond step counting

Most wearables count activity. This project asks a harder question: is the movement healthy, harmful, accessible, or forced?

For disability, more steps are not always better. The goal is sustainable functional mobility, not conformity to walking.
