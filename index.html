INDIVIDUAL WITHIN-PERSON MOBILITY CASE STUDY

HandicapSkater

One person's long-running record of how walking and controlled inline skating can function very differently in the same body.

After pelvic and sacroiliac-joint injuries, walking became painful and difficult to sustain. Inline skates became a mobility strategy that connected movement, transportation, and daily access.

Wearable records, routes, episodic measurements, repeated protocols, photographs, and documentary history now make that lived comparison inspectable.

What Looks Like a Stunt Is the Access Story

A short public video shows the adaptation in one scene: shopping on skates, rolling to a motorcycle, and riding away without changing footwear.

What can look like performance is also a response to walking difficulty, balance, the burden of bending to change footwear, transportation barriers, and more than thirty years of adapting movement to real environments.

Appearance alone cannot show what the aid enables or what removing it would cost.

THE MISSING SENSOR IS CONTEXT

Wearables Measure Signals. Mobility Intelligence Must Explain Them.

Heart rate alone cannot tell whether a person was walking, skating, riding, waiting, or recovering. HRV can describe different states depending on when it was measured, what preceded it, and which source produced it.

Longitudinal recovery and episodic sessions answer different questions. Route, transportation mode, within-person baselines, and source provenance can turn isolated measurements into context-preserving mobility intelligence.

The challenge for wearable, digital-health, sensor, transportation, and AI teams is to preserve those distinctions while making the result useful to a human reviewer.
