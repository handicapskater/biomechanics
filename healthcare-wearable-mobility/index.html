Wearable Mobility Evidence for Individualized Accommodation

Most wearables count activity. HandicapSkater asks a harder question: is the movement healthy, harmful, accessible, or forced?

This is a real world wearable health mobility evidence case study showing how route data, HR/HRV, recovery signals, HRV/RRI testing, accelerometer evidence, Fractal Stability Index, Comparable Similarity Score, and source linked activity labels can help document functional mobility when ordinary categories do not describe a person's lived reality.

The reusable idea

This is not a diagnostic product, a single-metric pain claim, or a claim of clinical validation. It is a research and product development framework for organizing user-controlled mobility evidence so reviewers can evaluate function, burden, and accommodation context instead of guessing from appearance.

Mobility is functional

The central question is not whether movement looks familiar. The question is whether it lets a disabled person move safely, sustainably, and accessibly.

Labels need context

Walking, exercise, recreation, transportation, wheelchair use, and ParaTransit labels can hide the lived mobility burden unless source context is preserved.

Evidence needs boundaries

Wearable and route evidence can support individualized review, but no single metric proves pain, disability, diagnosis, or legal entitlement by itself.

The problem

Mobility limitations are often invisible to systems that expect standard categories: walking, wheelchair use, exercise, transportation, or recreation.

For some disabled people, more steps are not better. The goal is sustainable functional mobility, not conformity to walking.

The healthcare opportunity is to help people explain functional mobility with source linked evidence, not to force every body into the same activity model.

The case study

HandicapSkater documents a long term disability accommodation record involving prosthetic inline skates used as a non-traditional mobility aid. The project combines lived experience, biomechanics, legal-access records, GPS route maps, wearable data, targeted sensor testing, and reproducible evidence organization.

Evidence architecture

The evidence layer is designed to preserve source roles instead of flattening every signal into one overclaimed score.

Wearable data

WHOOP-style wearable data provides long-history HR/HRV, strain, recovery, and activity label context.

GPS route data

Strava GPS route data provides route, distance, duration, elevation, speed, and repeated physical therapy skating context.

Targeted sensor testing

Kubios / Polar H10 testing provides HRV/RRI, accelerometer, Fractal Stability Index, and Comparable Similarity Score evidence.

The integrated evidence layer preserves source context, separates baseline and recovery records from burden comparisons, and avoids single-metric claims.

What this does not claim

This brief does not claim medical diagnosis, clinical validation, single-metric proof of pain, platform endorsement, agency endorsement, court endorsement, or a guaranteed legal outcome. It frames wearable and route evidence as support for individualized human review.

Why this matters for healthcare and wearable health teams

A user-controlled mobility evidence layer could help people organize wearable data, route data, targeted sensor sessions, and accommodation events into privacy preserving summaries for clinicians, accessibility teams, agencies, employers, and courts.

The opportunity is not to turn wearables into courtroom machines. The opportunity is to help people document functional mobility safely, transparently, and usefully.
